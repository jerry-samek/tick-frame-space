# RAW 121 — Rotational Gradient Inversion and the Antigravitation Threshold

## 0. Status

**Theoretical prediction. Not experimentally validated.**

This document derives a falsifiable prediction from first principles of the
gamma-field ontology (RAW 076–094). No empirical confirmation exists at the
time of writing. The prediction is stated precisely so that it can be tested
or refuted.

---

## 1. Background

### 1.1 Gravity as Gradient

In the gamma-field model (RAW 082, 083), gravity is not a force. It is a
geometric consequence of gamma-field curvature around a mass entity.

A massive object produces a **gamma well** — a region of increased field
density. Surrounding entities experience a gradient and hop toward the well
center. This is observed as gravitational attraction.

### 1.2 Magnetic Field as Tangential Curvature

RAW 086 establishes that rotation of a charged entity produces **tangential
deformation** of its gamma well — the magnetic field. This is not separate
from the gravitational well; it is its angular component.

### 1.3 Well vs Hill

RAW 091 establishes the dual geometry: a gamma **well** produces attraction;
a gamma **hill** produces repulsion. Under normal conditions, massive objects
produce wells. The question is whether a hill can be induced artificially.

---

## 2. The Core Prediction

**A sufficiently large rotational velocity of a heavy, electromagnetically
charged metal object will cause the local gamma gradient to invert — converting
a well into a hill — producing a measurable reduction or reversal of the
gravitational force on the object.**

---

## 3. Derivation

### 3.1 Connector Production Rate

Every entity continuously deposits gamma connectors into the surrounding field.
The rate of connector production is a function of:

- mass (depth of the gamma well)
- electromagnetic charge state (tangential curvature amplitude from RAW 086)
- rotational velocity ω

Formally:

    R_production = f(m, q, ω)

Where m = mass, q = electromagnetic charge state, ω = angular velocity.

### 3.2 Connector Absorption Rate

Surrounding field nodes absorb connectors at a finite rate determined by:

- local field density
- proximity to other massive objects
- substrate expansion rate

Formally:

    R_absorption = g(ρ_field, proximity, expansion)

### 3.3 Equilibrium and Inversion Condition

Under normal conditions:

    R_production ≤ R_absorption → gamma well → gravitational attraction

When rotational velocity exceeds a critical threshold ω_c:

    R_production > R_absorption → connector surplus → local field pressure

The surplus connectors cannot be absorbed. They accumulate outward from the
object. The local gradient inverts:

    ω > ω_c → gamma hill → gravitational repulsion

### 3.4 Role of Heavy Metal

A heavy metal is required for two reasons:

1. **Mass** — greater gamma well depth → larger baseline connector production.
   The inversion threshold is easier to cross.

2. **Electromagnetic conductivity** — a rotating electromagnetically active
   medium generates tangential curvature (magnetic well, RAW 086) that
   amplifies connector production through field coupling. The combined
   radial + tangential production exceeds what either mechanism alone
   could achieve.

A light or non-conductive rotating object would require impractically high ω
to cross the inversion threshold.

### 3.5 Critical Angular Velocity

The inversion occurs when:

    f(m, q, ω_c) = g(ρ_field, proximity, expansion)

This defines a critical angular velocity ω_c that depends on:

- the mass of the rotating body
- its electromagnetic charge density
- the local field environment

**Prediction: ω_c decreases as mass and charge density increase.**

A sufficiently massive, sufficiently charged rotating body has a lower
inversion threshold. At laboratory scale, this threshold may be very high.
At large scale, it may be physically achievable.

## 4. Expected Observable Effects

If the gradient inversion occurs, the following effects are predicted:

### 4.1 Weight Reduction

A scale measurement of the rotating object should show reduced weight
proportional to the degree of gradient inversion.

### 4.2 Threshold Behavior

The effect should appear **abruptly above ω_c**, not gradually. Below the
threshold, no measurable effect. Above it, a discontinuous change.

This distinguishes gradient inversion from classical centrifugal effects,
which scale continuously with ω.

### 4.3 Directional Anisotropy

The inverted gradient is strongest along the rotational axis. The effect
should be **directional**, not isotropic. Maximum repulsion above and below
the rotation plane; minimum effect at the equator.

### 4.4 Field Asymmetry Signature

If ω is high enough to partially invert the gradient but not fully, a mixed
state exists: gravitational attraction in some directions, repulsion in others.
This asymmetry should be detectable with sufficiently sensitive gravimeters
(sub-0.01 nm/s² sensitivity).

---

## 5. Null Result Condition

The prediction is falsified if:

- Weight measurement shows no reduction at any achievable ω for a
  heavy electromagnetically charged metal
- The effect scales continuously with ω (classical centrifugal, not
  gradient inversion)
- The effect is isotropic (same in all directions)
- No threshold behavior is observed

A null result would constrain the model: either ω_c is not physically
achievable at laboratory scale, or the connector production/absorption
mechanism does not operate as described.

## 6. Relationship to Existing Experimental Literature

Several anomalous experimental results in the literature are consistent with
this prediction but do not confirm it:

- Rotating superconductors show anomalous weight reduction in some
  measurements (Podkletnov, 1992). Not independently replicated at
  sufficient sensitivity.
- Spinning gyroscopes show small anomalous precession effects in some
  experiments. Explanations contested.

These results are noted for context. They do not constitute evidence for
the gamma-field gradient inversion mechanism.

## 7. Open Questions

1. What is the precise functional form of R_production(m, q, ω)?
   The model does not yet specify this analytically.

2. Is there a maximum inversion depth? Can R_production arbitrarily exceed
   R_absorption, or does the field self-regulate?

3. What happens to the inverted gradient when rotation stops?
   Instantaneous recovery or hysteresis?

4. Is the effect additive for multiple co-rotating charged masses?

These questions cannot be answered without either analytical derivation
from substrate axioms or experimental data.
