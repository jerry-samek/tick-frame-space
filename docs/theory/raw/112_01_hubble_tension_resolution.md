## Abstract

The Hubble tension is the observation that two independent measurements of the universe's
expansion rate give conflicting results: measurements using the early universe (CMB) give
H₀ ≈ 67 km/s/Mpc, while measurements using nearby galaxies and supernovae give
H₀ ≈ 73 km/s/Mpc. As of 2025, JWST has confirmed both measurements are correct —
eliminating the possibility of instrument error. Standard cosmology treats this as a crisis
because it assumes the expansion rate is a constant. The tick-frame framework dissolves the
tension immediately: expansion rate is not a constant. It is a running total of all movement
deposits ever made. Two measurements at different epochs measure different totals. Both are
correct. There is no tension.

---

## 1. The Standard Framing and Why It Creates a Problem

Standard cosmology embeds the Hubble constant H₀ as a fixed parameter in the ΛCDM model.
It represents the current expansion rate of the universe — one number, universally applicable.

Two methods measure it:

**Method 1 — Early universe (CMB):** Measures acoustic oscillations in the cosmic microwave
background. These are imprinted at recombination, ~380,000 years after the Big Bang. The
inferred H₀ ≈ 67 km/s/Mpc.

**Method 2 — Late universe (distance ladder):** Measures the recession velocity of nearby
galaxies using Cepheid variable stars and Type Ia supernovae as standard candles.
H₀ ≈ 73 km/s/Mpc.

The discrepancy is ~8–10%, far beyond measurement error. In 2024–2025, JWST independently
confirmed both measurements. The standard interpretation: the universe is expanding faster
than it should be, and the ΛCDM model is incomplete or wrong.

The assumption generating the crisis: **H₀ is a constant.**

## 2. The Framework Resolution

### 2.1 Expansion Rate Is a Running Total

From RAW 112 §2.5:

> *Expansion = accumulated connector extension from all hops ever taken.*

The expansion rate at any moment is the current rate of connector extension — which equals
the current total movement activity of all entities in the observable universe.

```
H(t) = total deposit-per-hop across all entities at time t
                        ÷
          total connector length at time t
```

This is not a constant. It is a rate that changes as the universe evolves.

### 2.2 Why Early and Late Universe Give Different Values

**Early universe (CMB epoch, t ≈ 380,000 years):**

The universe is hot, nearly uniform, and dominated by radiation. Most entities are simple —
photons, electrons, protons in a plasma. The total movement activity is high in absolute
terms (everything is moving fast) but the deposit structure is simple. The ratio of
deposits-per-hop to connector length gives H ≈ 67 km/s/Mpc at that epoch.

**Late universe (now, t ≈ 13.8 billion years):**

The universe has complexified enormously. Galaxies, stars, planets, life — all are running
deposit processes that did not exist at recombination. Total movement activity has grown as
structure formed. More entities, more complex entities, more deposits per tick. The ratio
gives H ≈ 73 km/s/Mpc at this epoch.

The CMB measurement reconstructs H at the recombination epoch and projects forward assuming
H is constant. The distance ladder measures H now. If H is not constant — if it evolves with
the universe's movement activity — then projecting the CMB value forward gives the wrong
answer. Both measurements are measuring the correct H for their epoch.

### 2.3 The Formal Statement

In the tick-frame framework, H is not H₀ (a constant). It is H(t) (a function of time):

```
H(t) ∝ Σ [deposit_per_hop(entity_i, t)] / Σ [connector_length(j, t)]
```

where the sums are over all entities i and all connectors j in the observable volume.

This function is monotonically increasing during the matter-dominated epoch as structure
formation increases total movement complexity. It may decelerate or reverse if structure
formation slows — but the current observational evidence (accelerating expansion) is
consistent with continued growth in total movement activity as the universe's deposit
structure deepens.

### 2.4 The Dark Energy Connection

Standard cosmology explains the accelerating expansion by introducing dark energy — a
cosmological constant Λ with negative pressure that drives accelerating expansion.

In the tick-frame framework, no dark energy parameter is needed. The "acceleration" is the
natural consequence of increasing total movement activity as the universe complexifies:

- Early universe: simple entities, lower deposit-per-hop rate relative to connector length
- Structure formation: galaxies, stars, planets increase total movement complexity
- Late universe: vastly more deposit activity than early universe → faster measured H(t)

The apparent acceleration is not a mysterious force pushing galaxies apart. It is the ledger
showing that the universe has been making more deposits per tick as it has complexified.

## 3. Connection to the Local Void Hypothesis

Independent of the tick-frame framework, a 2025 analysis proposed that the Milky Way may
sit within a billion-light-year underdensity — a local void with ~20% less matter than
the cosmic average. This would make local expansion measurements slightly faster than
the global average, potentially explaining the Hubble tension.

In the tick-frame framework: a local void is a region with lower gamma density. Lower gamma
density means the denominator in the growth rule is closer to 1, meaning local connectors
expand at closer to the maximum rate H. Higher local H → faster measured expansion locally.

The local void hypothesis and the framework are compatible and mutually reinforcing:
- The local void accelerates expansion locally (less gamma → faster connector growth)
- The framework explains why the global H has also increased since recombination
  (more movement deposits → higher global rate)

Both effects point in the same direction. The Hubble tension may reflect both a genuine
epoch-dependent increase in H AND a local void bias in distance ladder measurements.

## 4. The Testable Prediction

The framework makes a specific quantitative prediction that distinguishes it from a
cosmological constant model:

**H(t) should increase monotonically with cosmic time during the matter-dominated epoch,
tracking the growth of structural complexity.**

This predicts:
- H measured at z = 2 (young galaxies) < H measured at z = 0.5 (mature galaxies) < H₀
- The rate of H increase should correlate with the rate of galaxy and star formation
- H should plateau or decrease if/when structure formation slows

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) 2024 data — which found evidence that
dark energy may be evolving (weakening) over time — is consistent with this prediction.
A weakening "dark energy" in standard cosmology maps to a decreasing rate of increase in
H(t) as the universe's structure formation slows. Same phenomenon, different vocabulary.

---

## 5. Summary

The Hubble tension is not a tension. It is two correct measurements of H at two different
epochs. The apparent contradiction arises only under the assumption that H is constant.

In the tick-frame framework:
- H(t) = running total of movement deposits ÷ total connector length
- H grows as the universe complexifies and total movement activity increases
- CMB measures H at recombination — lower, because the universe was simpler
- Distance ladder measures H now — higher, because structure has added deposit activity
- No dark energy parameter needed
- No modification to gravity needed
- No tension

The "crisis in cosmology" dissolves under one substitution: replace the constant H₀ with
the running total H(t). Everything else follows from RAW 112 §2.5.
